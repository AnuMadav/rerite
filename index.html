  • Self-preferencing is an anti-competitive practice that involves actions by an undertaking which are designed to favour its own products or services over those of its competitors. Self-preferencing is often described as a subset of a broader type of conduct, known as leveraging.
  • This involves the use of power in one market to strengthen a position in another market. An undertaking with a dominant position in the market, may engage in self-preferencing in order to enhance its own market position
  • To prevent or inhibit the entry/expansion of other competitors. In other words, self-preferencing by dominant undertakings may produce exclusionary effects. Some of the notable self-preferencing practices include tying or bundling arrangements; discriminatory conduct and refusal to supply good or services. Self-preferencing is central to contemporary competition law discussions, in particular in digital markets.
